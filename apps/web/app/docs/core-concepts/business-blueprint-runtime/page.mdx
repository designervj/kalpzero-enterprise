# Business settings and modules

Kalp changes from business to business through settings, not through separate codebases. These settings decide which modules a business sees, what its navigation looks like, and how its public website should feel.

<Callout title="Internal name" tone="brand">
  In the codebase, this group of settings is called the `Business Blueprint`. In plain language, it is the main business settings document.
</Callout>

<h2 id="what-these-settings-control">What these settings control</h2>

The business settings document controls the parts of Kalp that a business should experience differently.

<DocsCardGrid>
  <DocsCard title="Business words and labels">
    A business can use its own labels and names so the dashboard language matches the business model.
  </DocsCard>
  <DocsCard title="Menus and routes">
    Navigation items and available routes can change from one business to another.
  </DocsCard>
  <DocsCard title="Theme and colors">
    A business can have its own public and admin look without needing a different frontend codebase.
  </DocsCard>
  <DocsCard title="Enabled modules">
    Kalp can show hotel tools to one business and commerce tools to another using the same product.
  </DocsCard>
</DocsCardGrid>

<h2 id="navigation-and-modules">Navigation and modules</h2>

This is where Kalp decides what each business can use.

- enabled modules decide which product areas appear in the dashboard
- navigation rules decide which menu items are shown
- feature flags allow controlled rollout without changing the app itself

That is why two businesses can log into the same product and still see different tools.

<h2 id="look-and-feel">Look and feel</h2>

Kalp separates the shared app from the business-specific look and feel.

- the app stays shared
- the business colors, wording, and navigation can change
- the public website and admin area can each have their own theme settings

This gives each business a clear identity without making Kalp harder to maintain.

<h2 id="why-kalp-uses-settings">Why Kalp uses settings</h2>

If every business had its own forked app, simple fixes would become expensive and slow. Kalp avoids that by keeping the code shared and moving business-specific changes into settings and content.

<blockquote>
  Change the app once, and every business can benefit. Change a business setting once, and only that business changes.
</blockquote>
